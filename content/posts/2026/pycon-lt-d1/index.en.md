Yesterday was PyCon LT (Lithuania) Day 1: Python Day.

This is my second time attending PyCon LT, and yesterday's highlight was a "breaking the fourth wall" community interaction experience.

Here are the two things that impressed me the most yesterday.

---

## 01. From GitHub Follow to Face-to-Face Meeting: My "Real-Life Encounter" with Airflow Core Committer Jarek

Among yesterday's speakers, the most familiar to many developers was probably Airflow's core committer, **Jarek**.

His talk was titled: How to Modernize a Complex Monorepo Project Using `uv` and `prek`.

He had previously shared this topic online; interested readers can refer to [this podcast](https://talkpython.fm/episodes/show/123/modernizing-monorepos-with-uv-and-prek).

However, for me, the "bravest" thing I did yesterday was to proactively chat with him during a coffee break.

### Successfully Met My Idol

I've been following Jarek for a long time, often seeing his active presence in PyPA and GitHub Issues.

I also previously wrote an [article about prek](2025/pre-commit-competitors), and he is one of the key figures heavily promoting `prek`. (On a side note: I later retweeted a post by Jo, the author of prek, on X, telling him that Jarek mentioned `prek` again at PyCon LT. To my surprise, Jo not only liked and replied but also followed me back – double the happiness!)

**Several interesting details from our conversation:**

*   **Finding an entry point:** To avoid awkward small talk, I specifically chose to approach him about 10 minutes before the next session began. When he asked about me, I said I was an amateur open-source contributor and mentioned [cpp-linter](https://github.com/cpp-linter/cpp-linter), which I maintain.
*   **Self-promotion:** Jarek seemed more interested when he heard that Apache was also using the cpp-linter project. He asked for my GitHub ID and had me type it directly into his computer. After seeing my contribution heatmap, he commented that I had made quite a few contributions. (As a side note, I am currently ranked first among open-source contributors in Lithuania, which I consider a small achievement from "rolling up my sleeves" overseas.)
*   **About Airflow:** He quickly glanced at my contribution history and asked if I had contributed to Airflow. I frankly replied that I don't use it much currently but plan to delve deeper into it later.
*   **The prek connection:** We also discussed the origin of `prek`'s name (originally `prefligit`) and its author, Jo.

Finally, I asked if we could take a photo together, and he readily agreed. Afterward, I also sent him the photo via email.

This experience of connecting offline after following someone online was quite interesting. Over the next two days, if I encounter other prominent figures I've followed on GitHub for a long time, I'll continue my "fan-chasing" efforts.

---

## 02. Mozilla's Engineering Taste: A High-Quality Presentation on Toolchains

Another afternoon session that kept me fully focused and made me pause my "Vibe Coding" was by Tadas Korris – he presented **how Mozilla selects and uses its Python toolchain**.

### This presentation left a deep impression on me:

1.  **High Engineering Taste:**
    He didn't just list tools; he demonstrated what Mozilla uses across the entire chain, from Typing, Linter, Testing, CI/CD, Monitoring, to DevOps. I've encountered most of these tools, but it was the first time I heard them presented so systematically, which was very satisfying.

2.  **Practical Experience from the Front Lines:**
    As a local developer, Tadas – like many Europeans whose English is their second or even third language – spoke incredibly well and fluently, articulating his points clearly. He shared a lot of practical experience and pitfalls from his time at Mozilla, and this content was extremely valuable.

An interesting detail was that Jarek, who usually codes throughout, seemed to be listening very attentively during this presentation. After the talk, he was the first to raise his hand to ask a question, and the two then had a face-to-face discussion.

There was a sense of "resonance between masters," highlighting the shared understanding and pursuit of **Engineering Excellence** among top developers.
